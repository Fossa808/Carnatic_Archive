Grade 1

  • Nada:

    A musical sound.

  • Sruthi:

    The smallest perceptible interval of pitch.

  • Svara:

    A musical note or pitch. The seven fundamental notes are Sa, Re, Ga, Ma, Pa, Dha, Ni. They are the short forms of Shadjam, Rishabham, Gandharam, Madhyamam, Panchamam, Dhaivatam, and Nishadam, respectively.

  • Sthayi:

    Sthayi refers to an octave or a span of seven notes (svaras).

  • Ragas:

    A Raga is a melodic framework that provides a foundation for composition. It has specific rules for ascending and descending scales and characteristic musical motifs.

    • Janaka Ragas (Melakarta Ragas):

      They are the 72 fundamental or "parent" scales. They are characterized by having all seven notes (Svaras) in both the ascending (Arohanam) and descending (Avarohanam) scales. Each Janaka raga has a unique pattern of notes, and they are the basis for all other ragas in the system.

    • Janya Ragas (Melajanyam Ragas):

      These are ragas derived from Janaka ragas by varying or omitting notes from the parent scale. Janya ragas often have fewer than seven notes in their ascending or descending scales. They can be classified based on the number of notes they contain (e.g. Auḍava ragas have five notes, Shadava ragas have six notes, Sampoorna ragas have all seven notes).

  • Talas:

    Tala is a rhythmic structure in Carnatic music that is made up of a repeating cycle of beats. It is a fundamental part of Carnatic music as it provides a blueprint for musicians to follow as they perform a composition.

    • Sapta Talas:

      The seven fundamental talas that form the basis for rhythmic structure and improvisation in music. They are Dhruva (14 beats), Matya (10 beats), Rupakam (6 beats), Eka (4 beats), Jhampa (7 beats), Triputa (8 beats), and Ata (12 beats).

    • Shadangas:

      Refers to the six aspects used for accurate musical time reckoning, which are Anudrutam, Drutam, Laghu, Guru, Plutam, and Kakapadam

      • Anudrutam: The smallest unit of time.

      • Drutam: A unit of time twice the length of Anudrutam.

      • Laghu: A unit of time that is a combination of Anudrutam and Drutam.

      • Guru: A unit of time twice the length of Laghu.

      • Plutam: A unit of time that is a combination of Laghu and Guru.

      • Kakapadam: A unit of time that is a combination of Guru and Plutam.

    • Chapu Talas:

      The rhythmic cycles that fall outside the standard "Suladi Sapta Tala" system, representing syncopated time measures where the beat falls on an unaccented place, with variations like Misra Chapu (7 beats) (Thakita Thaka Dhimi), Khanda Chapu (5 beats), Tisra Chapu (3 beats), and Sankeerna Chapu (9 beats). This Tala has evolved from South Indian folk music and even the name has Tamil roots from the word Chaippu, meaning slanting or sloping. The two parts (angas) has increasing counts such 2+3, 3+4 or 4+5.

  • Composers:

    • Purandaradasar:

      Sri Purandara Dasa, was a 15th-century saint, musician and composer. He was born in Karnataka in 1484 as Srinivasa Nayaka. He’s known as the “Father of Carnatic Music” for establishing a structured method for teaching Carnatic Music and was a prominent figure in the Haridasa movement. He is said to have composed over 475,000 songs although only about 1,000 have survived.

    • Muthuswami Dikshitar

      Sri Muthuswami Dikshitar was born in 1775 in the town of Thiruvarur, which is near Thanjavur. He is among the Trinity of Carnatic Music. He was a composer, singer, poet and veena player. His compositions are typically known for their poetic renditions of Hindu Gods/Temples and capturing the essence of the raga forms through the vainika style (emphasizes gamakam). His signature name and mudra are both Guruguha.

  • Composers:

    • Thirugnanasambanthar:

      Also known as Sambandar, Thirugnanasambanthar was a 7th Century Shaiva poet-saint. He was born in Sirkazhi, near Chidambaram. He composed 16,000 hymns, out of which 384 have survived. His devotional songs laid the foundation for the development of Carnatic Music and its emphasis on devotional themes. He is one of the 63 Nayanmars.

    • Arunagirinathar:

      Arunagirinathar was a 4th Century Tamil Shaiva poet-saint. He was born in Thiruvannamalai. He composed 16,000 songs out of which 1,334 have survived. Some of the works he composed are the Thiruppukal, Tiruvakuppu, and Kandar Alangaram. His songs portray a way to a life of virtue and set the tone for musical worship.

Grade 3

  • Lakshana Githam:

    It is a type of song that serves as a musical glossary of a particular Raga. The Sahitya of the Gitam describes the various characteristics of the raga in which it is composed: Swaras, Graha, Nyasa, Amsa, Mela.

  • Swarajathi:

    It is a composition that is typically used as a stepping stone to learn pieces like Varnams. It typically features a Pallavi, an Anupallavi, and one or more Charanams. It has both Swaram and Sahithyam. Compositions are typically about bhakti, love, and courage.

  • Varnam:

    It is a fundamental form of Carnatic Music, comprising of Sahithyam and Swara passages, including Pallavi, Anupallavi, Muktayi Swara, Charanam, and Chittai Swara. There are different types of Varnams, such as Taana Varnam, Pada Varnam, Daru Varnam, and Ragamalika Varnam, and they also come in different talams. Varnams encapsulate the key features of a raga, and helps to develop voice culture and proper pitch and control of rhythm.

  • Kriti:

    A kriti (कृति) is generally a longer and more complex form that is the core of a Carnatic Music Concert. The term kriti in Sanskrit translates to "creation". They are known for their delicate blend of text, melody, and rhythm. The structure of kritis in general is a Pallavi, Anupallavi, and one or more charanams.

  • Gamaka:

    It refers to a specific type of melodic movement used to embellish a Svara. It helps shape the character and beauty of a Raga.

  • Vadi

    The most important and dominant note in a raga, often considered the "root" of the scale. This note defines the raga's character, and is frequently used in melodic phrases.

  • Samvadi

    The second most important note, typically the fifth or fourth above the Vadi. It's a "supporting note" that creates a strong relationship with the Vadi, often used in melodic phrases.

  • Anuvadi

    All other notes in the raga, excluding the Vadi and Samvadi are the Anuvadi.

  • Vivadi

    Notes that aren't present in the raga's arohanam and avarohonam. Skillful musicians use them carefully to create tension and dissonance.

Grade 4

  • Classification of Musical Instruments:

    • String (Chordophones): 
      Veena - A long-necked plucked string instrument.
Violin - Used for melodic accompaniment.
Gottuvadyam - A string instrument played by rubbing a bow.
Tambura - A drone instrument, providing a continuous harmonic background.
Sitar - Plucked string instrument primarily used in Hindustani Music.
    • Wind (Aerophones): 
      Flute - A musical instrument that produces sound when air is blown across a hole. It usually has 8 finger holes.
Nadaswaram - A double-reed wind instrument used in temple music.
    • Percussion (Idiophones & Membranophones): 
      Mridangam - The main percussion instrument, providing rhythmic accompaniment.
Ghatam - An earthenware pot struck to produce a low-pitched sound.
Kanjira - A hand-held percussion instrument, used for rhythmic accompaniment.
Morsing - A frame drum with a membrane.
Thavil - A double-headed drum used in temple music and folk traditions.
Tabla - A pair of hand drums. While primarily Hindustani, also used in Carnatic.
