Grade 1

  • Nada:

    A musical sound.

  • Sruthi:

    The smallest perceptible interval of pitch.

  • Svara:

    A musical note or pitch. The seven fundamental notes are Sa, Re, Ga, Ma, Pa, Dha, Ni. They are the short forms of Shadjam, Rishabham, Gandharam, Madhyamam, Panchamam, Dhaivatam, and Nishadam, respectively.

  • Sthayi:

    Sthayi refers to an octave or a span of seven notes (svaras).

  • Ragas:

    A Raga is a melodic framework that provides a foundation for composition. It has specific rules for ascending and descending scales and characteristic musical motifs.

    • Janaka Ragas (Melakarta Ragas):

      They are the 72 fundamental or "parent" scales. They are characterized by having all seven notes (Svaras) in both the ascending (Arohanam) and descending (Avarohanam) scales. Each Janaka raga has a unique pattern of notes, and they are the basis for all other ragas in the system.

    • Janya Ragas (Melajanyam Ragas):

      These are ragas derived from Janaka ragas by varying or omitting notes from the parent scale. Janya ragas often have fewer than seven notes in their ascending or descending scales. They can be classified based on the number of notes they contain (e.g. Auḍava ragas have five notes, Shadava ragas have six notes, Sampoorna ragas have all seven notes).

  • Talas:

    Tala is a rhythmic structure in Carnatic music that is made up of a repeating cycle of beats. It is a fundamental part of Carnatic music as it provides a blueprint for musicians to follow as they perform a composition.

    • Sapta Talas:

      The seven fundamental talas that form the basis for rhythmic structure and improvisation in music. They are Dhruva (14 beats), Matya (10 beats), Rupakam (6 beats), Eka (4 beats), Jhampa (7 beats), Triputa (8 beats), and Ata (12 beats).

    • Shadangas:

      Refers to the six aspects used for accurate musical time reckoning, which are Anudrutam, Drutam, Laghu, Guru, Plutam, and Kakapadam

      • Anudrutam: The smallest unit of time.

      • Drutam: A unit of time twice the length of Anudrutam.

      • Laghu: A unit of time that is a combination of Anudrutam and Drutam.

      • Guru: A unit of time twice the length of Laghu.

      • Plutam: A unit of time that is a combination of Laghu and Guru.

      • Kakapadam: A unit of time that is a combination of Guru and Plutam.

    • Chapu Talas:

      The rhythmic cycles that fall outside the standard "Suladi Sapta Tala" system, representing syncopated time measures where the beat falls on an unaccented place, with variations like Misra Chapu (7 beats) (Thakita Thaka Dhimi), Khanda Chapu (5 beats), Tisra Chapu (3 beats), and Sankeerna Chapu (9 beats). This Tala has evolved from South Indian folk music and even the name has Tamil roots from the word Chaippu, meaning slanting or sloping. The two parts (angas) has increasing counts such 2+3, 3+4 or 4+5.

  • Composers:

    • Purandaradasar:

      Sri Purandara Dasa, was a 15th-century saint, musician and composer. He was born in Karnataka in 1484 as Srinivasa Nayaka. He’s known as the “Father of Carnatic Music” for establishing a structured method for teaching Carnatic Music and was a prominent figure in the Haridasa movement. He is said to have composed over 475,000 songs although only about 1,000 have survived.

    • Muthuswami Dikshitar

      Sri Muthuswami Dikshitar was born in 1775 in the town of Thiruvarur, which is near Thanjavur. He is among the Trinity of Carnatic Music. He was a composer, singer, poet and veena player. His compositions are typically known for their poetic renditions of Hindu Gods/Temples and capturing the essence of the raga forms through the vainika style (emphasizes gamakam). His signature name and mudra are both Guruguha.
